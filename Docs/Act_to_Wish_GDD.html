Programme · No. 1

High concept

Win a wish from the clown who runs the theatre — if you can perform three plays well enough to earn it.

A regular person, desperate for one wish, is offered a deal by the Impresario — an evil clown-wizard who runs a mysterious theatre. Choose a wish, sign his Joker card, and perform three plays. Each play pulls you onto a stage in third person while an audience — visible in the theatre, then heard once the curtain rises — cheers your brilliance and boos your failure, scoring every move. Earn a high enough rating across all three acts and your wish comes true. Fall short, and the clown collects what he is owed.

The pitch in one lineYou sign a clown-wizard’s Joker card, pick a wish, and perform three plays — parrying, fighting, and showing off for an audience whose cheers are your score — to learn whether your wish comes true.

Programme · No. 3

Design pillars

These three statements settle every design argument. If a feature does not serve a pillar, it gets cut.

I

The stage justifies the smallness

Every act is a theatrical set — small, stylized, deliberately artificial. This is the art direction, not a compromise. We never apologize for scope; we lean into it.

II

The audience is the game

The crowd is seen in the theatre, then heard once a play begins. Their cheers and boos are the player’s score, their feedback, and the emotional core. Everything you do is for the audience.

III

One mechanic, re-shaped each act

A single parry/combat system is the heart and the scoring engine. Each act changes its shape — the threat, the input, the payoff — never a whole new game. That is how six people ship in a month and how each act still feels fresh.

Programme · No. 4

Story & narrative frame

Premise

You are an ordinary person with a wish you would do anything to fulfill. The Impresario — an evil clown-wizard who runs a mysterious theatre — offers a bargain: choose your wish, sign his Joker card, and perform three plays well enough to earn it.

Structure

The game is a frame story. The hub is the dressing room, seen in first person, where you speak with the Impresario, choose your wish, and pick which act to perform next. The moment you step into an act, the camera pulls back to third person and the world becomes the set of a play — and the audience, present a heartbeat ago, vanishes from sight and remains only as voices reacting to your performance.

The wish

At the start you choose your wish from a short list the Impresario offers — no free text. It is referenced twice: read back mockingly as you sign the Joker card, and again at the end. A fixed list lets each wish drive an authored line in both the win and the lose ending, giving cheap variety and a reason to replay.

Programme · No. 5

Cameras

The game runs on two cameras, and the switch between them carries the whole fantasy of stepping onto a stage.

  • First person — the dressing room only. Choosing your wish, talking to the Impresario, picking the next act. Intimate and grounded; this is the quiet hub between performances.
  • Third person — the moment you enter an act. Over-the-shoulder and single-player (the camera feel of It Takes Two, not its co-op). Third person sells the performance: you watch your own character perform, mirroring the audience, and it makes the parry and its flourishes readable and crowd-pleasing.
Make the switch a moment
The first-to-third-person transition is the “stepping onto the stage” beat. Direct it — curtain, light bloom, a push-back of the camera as the crowd swells — rather than a hard cut. It is one of the cheapest, most memorable seconds in the game.

Programme · No. 7

Scoring — the parry system

Score is your rating, and the engine that drives it is the parry. Underneath, every parry is one system: a threat telegraphs, a timing window opens, and you either nail it (score + cheer), whiff it (boo), or get hit (damage → eventual respawn). Each act re-shapes that single system; a perfect parry is the headline cheer moment.

Score sourceEffect
Perfect parryBig score + a crowd cheer — the headline move
Riposte / reflected hitKill or damage off a parry — large score
Offense (melee or ranged)Chip damage and kills — steady score
CollectablesHidden, themed pickups — bonus score
Combo / chainConsecutive parries build a multiplier — feeds crowd mood
Miss / hit / deathMurmurs, boos, groans — score loss

Targeting

A soft lock-on keeps third-person parries readable — the riposte or deflected shot snaps to the nearest threat, with manual override. It makes an average player look cool, which is the whole point of a crowd watching you.

Death & respawn (diegetic)

There is no real death. Die and the stage resets to the last checkpoint, the crowd groans, your rating drops, and the play continues until you finish it.

Pickup items vs collectables

Two different systems: pickup items are physics props you can carry and use in the environment; collectables are hidden score pickups. Don’t conflate them.

Scoring & thresholds

Each act yields a star rating on a “review” screen; the three sum into a total; a single threshold decides the ending. Tune it so a careful run passes and a sloppy one fails.

Programme · No. 8

The audience system

The audience is visible in the theatre and dressing room, but the moment a play begins it vanishes from sight and is driven entirely by audio for the rest of the performance — our highest-impact, lowest-cost system, and the source of your scoring feedback.

  • Ambient murmur — a seamless looping “bed.”
  • Cheer / applause — on parries and flair. Boo / groan — on misses and deaths.
  • Gasps, laughs, swells — flavor.
Crowd mood
A single runtime value (0–1) rises with parries and falls with misses, cross-fading the ambient bed between “warm/engaged” and “cold/restless.” Wire the combo meter straight into it — now score, combo, and the “audience is the game” pillar are all one number.
Programme · No. 9

The three acts

Each act re-shapes the same parry/combat system, and the player is cast as a different lead each time. The comedic public-domain-flip framing for the plays is still the plan; the specific stories are being decided.

Act I · Tutorial

The knight

You play — a knight with a sword

New shape: directional sword parry

The parry
A regular, readable sword parry — block toward the side the attack comes from. The obvious shape for a tutorial.
Offense
Melee damage and kills also score; ripostes off a parry score big.
Setting / story
Medieval; the specific public-domain tale is still to be determined.
Purpose
Teaches the whole loop — parry, riposte, collect, cheer/boo, diegetic reset, soft lock-on.
Act II · New shape

Sci-fi

You play — lead to be determined

New shape: ranged bolt deflection

The parry
Non-melee — time a deflect on incoming energy bolts and aim the reflection back at the shooter. A radial pulse parry handles swarms.
Why it works
Same timing brain, new shape (ranged + aim) — it proves the one system generalizes beyond melee.
Setting / story
Sci-fi; the specific story is still to be determined.
Offense
Reflected bolts and direct fire score; collectables are themed energy cells / data cores.
Act III · The finale

To be determined

You play — lead to be determined

New shape: TBD — combine or escalate

Direction
The finale should combine or escalate the earlier shapes — a rhythmic or type-matched parry, or melee + ranged together — for the highest skill ceiling.
The finale
The highest-stakes performance, where everything the player has learned across the three acts comes together.
Setting / story
Theme and story still to be determined.

Programme · No. 11

Systems architecture

One rule above all
Systems talk through events, not direct references. Combat raises ParryLanded; Score, Crowd, and UI listen. This keeps every system testable in isolation and is what lets the team build the independent ones in parallel.
TierBuild whenSystems
0 — FoundationsStart now · parallelInput, HP, Score/Rating, Audio manager, UI manager, Interaction
1 — Movement & presentationOn Tier 0Movement, Camera (dual), Animation (third-person)
2 — Interactive layerOn the abovePickup items, Collectables, Dialogue, Targeting / lock-on, Crowd reaction
3 — Combat & enemiesIntegrativeCombat (modular parry), Enemy AI, Checkpoint / respawn
4 — OrchestrationSkeleton early, finish lastAct sequencer (“play script”), Game flow / state, Results + endings

Two systems are easy to forget and costly if left late: the Crowd reaction (it is your scoring feedback and your identity) and the Act sequencer (without it you have mechanics but no playable act).

Programme · No. 12

Art direction

  • Style — low-poly, stylized; theatrical sets with visible stage edges and painted backdrops. This is the licence for small scenes.
  • Third person changes the lift — you now need a player character model plus an animation set (idle, run, parry, riposte, hit, death) and a costume per act (knight, sci-fi, Act 3). Reuse one rig and the same animations across acts; that is your main scope control.
  • The Impresario — a key character model: a sinister clown-ringmaster, present in the dressing room.
  • Visible low-poly crowd in the theatre and dressing room (kept simple and repeated), which vanishes when a play begins.
  • Palette discipline — each act owns a limited palette (medieval, sci-fi, TBD). The theatre frame is neutral red-and-black.
  • Lighting carries quality — spend the “wow” budget on bold stage lighting and coloured fog.
Programme · No. 13

Audio direction

  • The crowd system is the priority audio task.
  • Music — a light theatrical score per act; swell on big parry/cheer moments.
  • Diegetic theatre sounds — curtain swish, lights powering up on death, the camera-shift sting.
  • The Impresario’s voice — the one consistent voice: a smooth, mocking clown, present in the dressing room.
Programme · No. 14

UI / UX

  • Wish selection — an opening screen: pick your wish from a list, then sign the Joker card.
  • Dressing room — a first-person interface to choose which act to perform.
  • In-act HUD — minimal: HP, score/combo, a crowd-mood indicator, and the soft lock-on reticle. The audience is the main feedback.
  • Rating screen — a theatrical “review” between acts: star rating + a one-line verdict.
  • Endings — the closing sequence is the signature UX moment; direct it as a sequence, not a menu.

Programme · No. 16

Scope & production plan

The most important section
One month, six people. Build the loop, not the content, first. And watch third-person animation — it is the sneaky cost that sinks third-person student projects.

Vertical-slice-first

Build all of Act 1 (the knight) end-to-end — dressing room → step onto stage → parry/combat loop → review → return — before touching Acts 2 and 3. Once that single loop sings, the other acts re-shape proven systems rather than building new ones.

Priorities (MoSCoW)

  • Must — dual camera, parry/combat, crowd system, HP, score, Act 1 fully playable, one ending.
  • Should — Acts 2 & 3, both endings, review screen, wish list.
  • Could — extra parry flair, pickup-item puzzles, per-act music.
  • Won’t (this month) — co-op, branching dialogue, full voice acting.

Four-week schedule

WeekDevelopersArtists

If you fall behind — the cut order

  • Cut Act 3’s new shape → reuse melee + ranged from Acts 1–2.
  • Cut Act 3 entirely → ship a two-act game; adjust the threshold.
  • Simplify Act 2 to deflect-only (drop the radial pulse).

A polished two-act game beats a broken three-act one. Decide cuts early, not in the final 48 hours.
